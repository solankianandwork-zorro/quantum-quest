    Lesson 1

    Bits vs Qubits 🪙

    Everything your phone and laptop do is built from tiny switches called bits. Quantum computers use something wilder: qubits.

    A bit = a light switch

    A normal bit is like a light switch. It's either OFF (we call it 0) or ON (we call it 1). Never both. Click the switch:

    A qubit = a spinning coin

    🪙 The spinning coin Flip a coin and let it spin on the table. While it's spinning, is it heads or tails? It's… kind of both! Only when you slam your hand on it does it become one. A qubit is like that spinning coin. While "spinning" it's a mix of 0 and 1, and it only picks one when you look (measure) it.

    Spin the quantum coin and then measure it. Try it a few times — what do you notice?

    💡 Why is this useful? One spinning coin isn't magic. But put many qubits together and they can explore tons of possibilities at once — that's the superpower we'll build toward.
    Lesson 2

    Superposition 🌗

    "Superposition" is the fancy word for the spinning coin — being a mix of 0 and 1 at the same time.

    🌗 Half-moon idea Think of a dimmer switch instead of an on/off switch. A qubit can be 100% "0", 100% "1", or anywhere in between — like 70% leaning toward 0 and 30% toward 1. Those percentages are its probabilities.

    Drag the slider to choose how the qubit "leans". The bars show the chance of getting 0 or 1 if you measured it.

    🎲 It really is random Here's the spooky part: even if you knew everything about the qubit, you still can't predict a single measurement. You only know the odds. The universe genuinely rolls dice!
    🔬 Experiment Set the slider to 50/50 and press "Measure 100 times". Did you get exactly 50 and 50? Probably not! Random things wobble around the average. Try it a few times.
    Lesson 3

    Measurement 👁️

    In the quantum world, looking changes things. This is the rule that trips everyone up — so let's see it happen.

    👁️ The slamming hand Remember the spinning coin? The moment you slam your hand down, the spinning stops and you get heads or tails. In quantum land we call that "the wavefunction collapses". After you measure a qubit, it's stuck on the answer you got — the superposition is gone.

    This qubit is in a perfect 50/50 superposition. Press Measure and watch: the bars jump to 100% on whatever you got, and they stay there. To get the mix back, press Reset.

    ⚠️ Big consequence You can't peek at a qubit "a little bit". Every measurement forces a full decision and erases the maybe. That's why programming a quantum computer is all about being clever before you measure.
    Lesson 4

    Quantum Gates 🎛️

    Gates are the buttons you press to change a qubit. They're how you actually "program" quantum computers.

    Meet three gates

    GateNicknameWhat it does
    XThe Flipper 🔄Turns 0 into 1 and 1 into 0 (like NOT).
    HThe Mixer 🌀Turns a plain 0 into a perfect 50/50 spinning coin.
    ZThe Twister ↩️Secretly twists the qubit's "phase" (we'll see why that matters in Lesson 8).

    Press gates and watch the probability bars react. Try H (makes a mix), then measure. Or press X to flip, then H… experiment!

    🤯 Try this! Press H once → you get 50/50. Now press H again → it goes back to 100% "0"! Two mixers cancel out. That "un-mixing" is called interference, and it's the real secret sauce of quantum computing (Lesson 8).
    Lesson 5

    The Bloch Sphere 🌐

    Scientists draw a qubit as an arrow inside a globe. It's the qubit's secret map — and it makes the gates make sense.

    🌐 The globe Imagine a globe. The North Pole means "definitely 0". The South Pole means "definitely 1". Any spot around the equator is a 50/50 mix. The qubit is an arrow pointing somewhere on this globe — gates just rotate the arrow.

    Press gates and watch the arrow spin around the globe. North = 0, South = 1, equator = mix.

    🧭 Now gates make sense X rotates the arrow to point the opposite way (0↔1). H swings it to the equator (a mix). Z spins it around the pole — which doesn't change your chances of 0 or 1, but changes the hidden "phase". Phase is invisible until two paths meet and interfere!
    Lesson 6

    Entanglement 👻

    Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" — and it's the wildest idea in all of physics.

    🧦 Magic socks Imagine two magic socks in two boxes. You mail one box to the Moon. When you open your box and see a left sock, you instantly know the Moon box has a right sock — even though neither sock "decided" until you looked! Entangled qubits are linked like that: measuring one instantly tells you about the other.

    Below are two entangled qubits in a "Bell pair". Each measurement is random — but the two qubits always match. Press Measure a bunch of times and watch the magic.

    🚫 No faster-than-light texting It feels like magic teleporting messages — but you can't actually send a signal this way, because each result is random. You only notice the link when you bring both results back together and compare. (Still incredibly useful for quantum computing!)
    Lesson 7

    Build a Circuit 🧩

    A quantum program is just a circuit: qubits travel along wires (left → right) and you drop gates on them like LEGO.

    🎼 Like sheet music Read it left to right, like music. Each qubit is a line. Each gate is a note you play on that line at a certain moment.

    How to use the builder: click a cell to cycle through gates: empty → H → X → Z → • (CNOT control) → empty. Put a • on the top wire and the builder links it to the bottom wire to make a CNOT. Press ▶ Run to simulate!

    🏆 Challenge: build a Bell pair Column 1: put H on qubit 0. Column 2: put a • (CNOT control) on qubit 0. Run it — you should see only 00 and 11, never 01 or 10. You just built entanglement yourself! 🎉
    Lesson 8

    Your First Quantum Trick: Interference ✨

    Here's where quantum computing stops being random luck and starts being cleverness. The trick is called interference.

    🌊 Waves cancelling Drop two stones in a pond. Where the ripples meet, sometimes they add up into a big wave, and sometimes they cancel out to flat water. Qubits are like waves too. A quantum algorithm is designed so the wrong answers cancel out and the right answer adds up — so when you finally measure, you almost always get the answer you want.

    The "guaranteed coin" demo

    Watch two H gates in a row. A normal random coin flipped twice is still random. But a quantum coin H-ed twice comes back to 0 every single time — because the "1" paths cancel and the "0" paths add up. Press Run many times: it's always 0.

    🧠 Why this is a big deal Famous algorithms use this exact idea:
    • Grover's search 🔍 — finds a needle in a haystack much faster by making the needle's wave grow.
    • Shor's algorithm 🔐 — could crack certain codes by using interference to find hidden patterns in numbers.
    You don't need the heavy math yet — just know the secret is "make the right answer loud, make wrong answers quiet."

    Step 2 — Your first real quantum program

    I've already written 4 ready-to-run programs for you in the python/ folder next to this course. Here's the simplest one:

    from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator

# Make a circuit with 1 qubit and 1 "measuring slot"
qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1)
qc.h(0)          # H gate = the Mixer (spinning coin!)
qc.measure(0, 0) # look at the qubit

# Run it 1000 times on a pretend quantum computer
result = AerSimulator().run(qc, shots=1000).result()
print(result.get_counts())   # roughly {'0': 500, '1': 500}
